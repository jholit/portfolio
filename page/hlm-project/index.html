HLM AI An AI Workspace Concept

Summary

HLM AI explores how product design can improve the experience of working with a large language model. While AI platforms can feel easy to start using, the experience can become harder to manage as more features, controls, and terminology are added around the core conversation.

This project focuses on the front-facing workspace experience: how people navigate, prompt, and follow a conversation with AI. Instead of asking people to adapt to the interface, HLM AI explores how the interface can better support what people are trying to do.

Examining the Problem

Large language models are powerful, but the product experience around them can still feel feature-led. Important actions may compete with secondary tools, terminology can assume technical knowledge, and controls that shape the interaction are not always placed where people naturally need them.

The sidebar became one of the clearest areas to reconsider. Rather than treating it as a simple menu, I approached it as the operational center of the workspace: the place where people understand what the product can do, where their work lives, and how to move between conversations, tools, and projects.

  • The workspace needed clearer separation between what the AI is capable of doing and what people are most likely trying to use it for.
  • Primary actions, workspace tools, and supporting features needed stronger hierarchy so they did not compete for the same level of attention.
  • Prompt controls needed to live closer to the moment of interaction, allowing people to adjust model behavior without leaving the flow of writing.
  • The conversation area needed clearer speaker identity, timestamps, and message structure so people could follow and reference exchanges more easily.

These issues shaped the direction of HLM AI. The goal was not to redesign every possible feature, but to focus on the parts of an AI workspace people interact with most: finding where to go, deciding what to ask, and understanding what comes back.

Product Design Solutions

The solution was to redesign HLM AI around the everyday moments that define the workspace experience. I focused on the aside panel, the prompter, and the active chat area because those are the places where people navigate, control, and understand their interaction with AI.

The redesign supports this direction through the following decisions:

  1. I reorganized the sidebar so it works as an operational center instead of a crowded list of menu items, separating custom HLM tools, workspace areas, conversations, and supporting actions more clearly.
  2. I used more direct terminology for key workspace areas so people can understand what each section is for without needing to decode product-specific or technical language first.
  3. I moved model and capability controls closer to the prompt field so people can adjust how they want HLM AI to respond at the moment they are preparing to interact with it.
  4. I reframed AI capability options around conversational intent, using labels such as Talkative, Contemplative, and Thinking to make response behavior easier to understand from a human perspective.
  5. I redesigned the active chat area to feel more like a familiar conversation, using clearer speaker identity, message structure, and timestamps so people can follow who said what and reference past exchanges more easily.

Measurement of Outcomes

HLM AI demonstrates how the experience around a large language model can become easier to understand when core interactions are organized around people instead of feature exposure. The redesigned workspace gives clearer priority to navigation, prompting, and conversation flow without pretending the prototype needs to solve every possible AI use case.

The current version establishes a focused foundation for the project: a clearer aside panel, a more useful prompting control area, and a conversation layout that makes the exchange between person and AI easier to follow. Future updates can expand the prototype, but this version already communicates the main design direction.
